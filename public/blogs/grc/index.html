Scaling Azure Governance: Policy-as-Code with GitOps

As cloud footprints grow, manual governance becomes impossible. Drift creeps in, costs spiral out of control, and security controls fall out of alignment. Here is how we built a declarative, automated framework to manage Azure Policy and Management Groups at enterprise scale.

Manual governance in the public cloud is a losing battle.

Configuring Azure Policy, setting up Management Groups, and assigning RBAC permissions through the Azure Portal works well when you have a handful of subscriptions. But when your organization scales to tens or hundreds of subscriptions, you quickly run into a wall. Setting things up manually breeds inconsistency, makes auditing a nightmare, and runs the risk of architectural drift.

The solution is to treat governance like software. By defining our infrastructure hierarchy, role assignments, and security controls in code, we can deploy, version, and audit them through automated CI/CD pipelines. This is Policy-as-Code with GitOps.

The Architecture: Decoupling Hierarchy and Policy

Our governance model is built on top of a highly modular Terraform project structure. We specifically designed it to decouple resource hierarchy from policy definitions and assignments. This separation of concerns ensures that the core organization structure remains stable while allowing policies to evolve rapidly. 

GitOps Azure Policies
├── management-groups/          # Management Group Hierarchy
├── management-group-rbac/      # Role-Based Access Control Module
├── policy-resources/           # Custom Policy Definitions (YAML parser)
├── initiatives-resources/      # Policy Initiatives (Sets)
├── assignments/               # Policy and Initiative Assignments
└── exemptions/               # Policy Exemptions

1. The Hierarchy Foundation

Everything begins at the Management Group layer. We map our enterprise organizational structure straight to the Azure Active Directory tenant root. The management-groups module establishes our core management group tree: 

Enterprise Root
├── Platform Services
│   ├── Identity
│   ├── Management
│   └── Connectivity
├── Landing Zones
│   └── Corporate
│       ├── Corporate Production
│       └── Corporate Non-Production
├── Sandboxes
└── Decommissioned

By creating nested management groups, we inherit permissions and policies down the tree. For instance, production workloads under Corporate Production automatically inherit strict auditing controls, while the Sandboxes management group remains a flexible, sandboxed sandbox environment.

2. Separating RBAC from Creation

In typical configurations, RBAC permissions are assigned directly in the code that creates the management groups. In our framework, we built a dedicated management-group-rbac module. This decouples who has access from the hierarchy itself, allowing cloud operations to manage global permissions (e.g., security teams having read-only access tenant-wide) and project-specific owner permissions independently.

Driving Policies with Declarative YAML

Writing Azure Policy rules in JSON directly inside Terraform is verbose and error-prone. To bridge this gap, our policy-resources module parses custom policy definitions stored as static, easy-to-read YAML files.

This is where the magic happens: developers and security teams can contribute policies in simple YAML formats, and our Terraform engine automatically translates them into first-class Azure resources.

Enterprise Guardrails: Core Initiatives in Action

We group our individual policy rules into logical bundles called Policy Initiatives. This allows us to assign complex control sets in a single action. Here are the core initiatives running in our environment:

1. FinOps & Cost Tagging

To prevent cost runs, we enforce strict tagging standards. If a resource group is created, tags like Business CI, Tech Env, Business Application, and Business CostCenter must be present.

  • Inheritance: Resources inside a resource group automatically inherit these tags if they aren't explicitly specified at creation.
  • Auto-Shutdown (ARM&D): In non-production environments, we look for schedule tags. VMs without override tags are automatically shut down during off-business hours, yielding a 40-60% reduction in non-prod compute costs.

2. Identity and Group-Based RBAC

Following the principle of least privilege, we forbid direct user access assignments. The identity-governance initiative actively audits and blocks direct user RBAC assignments, forcing teams to route permissions through Entra ID Groups mapped directly to management groups.

3. Private Endpoint DNS Governance

In a secure enterprise hub-and-spoke network, private endpoints must resolve to centralized private DNS zones. Our DNS governance initiative automatically handles this complex plumbing by registering private endpoints into corporate DNS zones and preventing developers from creating conflicting private DNS zones locally.

4. EU Data Sovereignty

To comply with GDPR and local data protection regulations, the eu-governance initiative restricts resource deployments strictly to European Azure regions (e.g., westeurope and northeurope). Any attempt to spin up infrastructure outside these borders is blocked by default (Deny mode).

The GitOps Pipeline: Continuous Integration & Deployment

Any change to management groups or policies follows a structured GitOps workflow:

Workflow Phase Action Automation/Verification
1. Authoring Developer edits variables, policy YAMLs, or modules in a feature branch. Local validation using task terraform:validate.
2. Pull Request A PR is opened to target the main branch. CI pipeline triggers terraform plan, showing exactly which policies will be created, updated, or destroyed.
3. Approval Governance & Security teams review the plan and approve the PR. Peer review verification.
4. Deployment Merge to main branch. Azure DevOps pipeline automatically applies changes (terraform apply) to the tenant.

Exemption Management: Handling the Exceptions Safely

No policy is absolute. There will always be a legitimate business need to bypass a guardrail (e.g., an application requiring a legacy region or a specific key rotation exemption).

Rather than turning off a policy globally, we use a dedicated exemptions module. Exemptions are defined as code with a mandatory expiration date, approval reference, and documented mitigation. Once the expiration date passes, the exemption expires, and the security policy automatically re-enforces itself.

"Exemptions are code, not handshakes. They must be version-controlled, time-bound, and peer-reviewed."

Conclusion: Shifting Governance Left

By converting our enterprise guardrails from PDF documents into a declarative GitOps repository, we changed the nature of cloud governance. Compliance is no longer a reactive audit exercise; it is integrated directly into the developer workflow. If a deployment violates an enterprise guardrail, the deployment fails at the pipeline stage, giving engineers immediate, actionable feedback.
