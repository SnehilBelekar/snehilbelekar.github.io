Europe Just Realized It Doesn't Own Its Own Data

The digital economy runs on infrastructure. When that infrastructure is controlled elsewhere, so is strategic power.

Europe just realized it doesn't own its own data.

80% of EU digital infrastructure runs on non-European technology. In Belgium alone, Microsoft controls 72% of cloud infrastructure.

That's not a vendor relationship. That's dependency.

And the US CLOUD Act means American authorities can legally access that data, even when it's stored on European soil.

The EU finally said: enough.

Just this month, the European Commission launched its Tech Sovereignty Package, the first time the EU has formally defined what digital sovereignty actually means.

They created measurable sovereignty scores (SEAL levels) for cloud providers. They awarded a EUR 180M sovereign cloud contract to European providers. France and Germany launched a joint task force to build truly European alternatives.

The message is clear: data is the new strategic asset, and keeping it nearshore isn't just a compliance checkbox, it's geopolitical power.

We're watching a digital border being drawn in real time.

The question for every organization today isn't "is my cloud cheap?" It's "do I actually control my data, or does someone else?"
